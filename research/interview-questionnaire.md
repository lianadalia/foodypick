# User Research Interview Guide
## Food Delivery App — Exploratory Interviews

**Project:** foodypick
**Date:** 2026-03-03
**Format:** Semi-structured 1:1 interview, 30–45 minutes
**Goal:** Understand real behaviours, decision-making, and friction points around food delivery — not to validate assumptions, but to surface what we don't yet know.

---

## Before You Start

**Interviewer notes:**

- This is a conversation, not a survey. Silence is fine — let the participant think.
- Your job is to listen, not to explain the product or fill quiet gaps.
- Never suggest an answer or confirm that something is a "problem" before they say so.
- If a participant gives a short answer, follow up with: *"Can you tell me more about that?"* or *"What happened next?"*
- Record with permission. Take minimal notes during — full attention on the participant.
- Aim for stories, not opinions. "Tell me about a time when..." yields richer data than "Do you think...?"

**Screener:** Participant has used at least one food delivery app in the past 3 months.

---

## Warm-up (5 min)

Before the five questions, spend a few minutes settling the participant with low-stakes context:

> *"Before we get into the questions, can you just tell me a bit about your week — do you tend to cook, eat out, or order in? Just generally."*

This loosens them up, surfaces their baseline relationship with food, and gives you context for interpreting everything that follows. It is not one of the five questions.

---

## The Five Questions

---

### Question 1 — The Last Time

> **"Walk me through the last time you ordered food delivery. Start from whatever made you decide to order — what was happening?"**

**What we're learning:**
The real-world context that triggers a delivery order: emotional state, time of day, who else was involved, what alternatives were considered and rejected. This is the foundation for understanding when and why people reach for the app.

**Follow-up probes:**
- *"What made you decide to order rather than cook or go out?"*
- *"How did you choose which app to open?"*
- *"How did you decide what to get? Walk me through that."*
- *"Was there a moment where you almost stopped and did something else instead?"*
- *"How did you feel when the food actually arrived?"*

**Watch for:**
Emotional language (tired, lazy, celebratory, overwhelmed), workarounds like comparing multiple apps, any moment of hesitation or friction they glossed over quickly — those are worth excavating.

---

### Question 2 — The Decision

> **"When you open a delivery app and you're not sure what you want — how do you figure it out? What do you actually do?"**

**What we're learning:**
The mental model and decision process for choosing a restaurant and dish. Most apps are built around browse-and-search, but real behaviour may be very different — reordering, asking someone, scrolling without intent, using a completely different trigger.

**Follow-up probes:**
- *"Do you usually have something in mind before you open the app, or does the app help you decide?"*
- *"What makes you stop scrolling and commit to a restaurant?"*
- *"Has the app ever shown you something you ended up ordering? How did that happen?"*
- *"Is there anything you always check before adding something to your cart?"*
- *"Do you ever give up and do something else? What makes you do that?"*

**Watch for:**
Whether people are driven by habit (same restaurants repeatedly) or discovery (genuinely browsing). Whether photos, ratings, ETA, or price is the actual deciding factor vs. what they say it is. Any workarounds — checking the restaurant's own Instagram, asking a friend.

---

### Question 3 — When It Went Wrong

> **"Tell me about a time when ordering delivery was frustrating or didn't go how you expected. What happened?"**

**What we're learning:**
Real failure modes, ranked by emotional impact. People's worst experiences reveal the moments of highest consequence — where trust breaks down and churn is most likely. The specific moment they name first is usually the one that stings most.

**Follow-up probes:**
- *"What did you do when that happened?"*
- *"Did you try to resolve it, or did you just accept it? Why?"*
- *"How did it affect whether you used that app or restaurant again?"*
- *"Has something like that happened more than once? Is it a pattern or a one-off?"*
- *"If you could change one thing about that experience, what would it have been?"*

**Watch for:**
The difference between what people complain about (often price or wait time) and what actually changes their behaviour (often a trust failure — wrong food, allergen error, unanswered complaint). Also note what they did or didn't do in response — complaint behaviour is a strong signal.

---

### Question 4 — Trust and Reliability

> **"Are there restaurants or apps you keep going back to? What keeps you coming back — what makes you trust them?"**

**What we're learning:**
The specific signals that build trust and loyalty. This is the inverse of question 3 — instead of failure, what does success feel like and what causes it? Trust is rarely about one feature; it's usually about a pattern of consistent small things.

**Follow-up probes:**
- *"What would make you try somewhere completely new that you've never ordered from before?"*
- *"Is there anything that would make you stop using a restaurant you already like?"*
- *"Do you read reviews? What are you actually looking for when you do?"*
- *"Have you ever recommended a restaurant to someone after ordering delivery from them? What made you do that?"*
- *"If a restaurant you trusted raised their prices, at what point would that change things for you?"*

**Watch for:**
Whether trust is built at the platform level (the app itself) or restaurant level. Whether consistency, speed, accuracy, or price drives loyalty. Whether social proof (reviews, word of mouth) plays a real role or is just a rationalisation.

---

### Question 5 — The Workaround

> **"Is there anything about ordering food delivery that you've found a way around — something that doesn't quite work how you need it to, so you've figured out your own way of doing it?"**

**What we're learning:**
Workarounds are the clearest signal of unmet needs. When a person builds a habit around compensating for something the app doesn't do, that gap is real and validated. These are often the most actionable insights in a session because they reveal what people have decided is worth the effort to fix.

**Follow-up probes:**
- *"How did you figure that out? Did someone show you, or did you discover it yourself?"*
- *"How often do you do this?"*
- *"Do you know anyone else who does something similar?"*
- *"If that workaround didn't work, what would you do instead?"*
- *"Is there something you wish the app just did, so you didn't have to do it yourself?"*

**Watch for:**
Workarounds that multiple participants share independently — those are strong candidates for product features. Also note the effort level: a workaround someone does every order is more significant than one they tried once. Screenshots, screenshots sent to friends, switching apps mid-session, calling the restaurant directly — all are gold.

---

## Closing (3 min)

End with an open door, not a closed question:

> *"Is there anything about ordering food delivery that we haven't talked about that you think is important — anything you'd want someone designing one of these apps to know?"*

This catches anything that didn't fit neatly into the five questions and often produces the session's most candid moment.

## What These Questions Are Designed Not To Do

- They do not ask *"Would you use a feature that..."* — hypothetical feature questions produce unreliable answers
- They do not ask *"How satisfied are you with..."* — satisfaction ratings flatten nuance
- They do not mention foodypick — this research is about behaviour, not product feedback
- They do not lead with problems — the participant decides what counts as a problem
